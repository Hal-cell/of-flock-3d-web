Flock

an experiment in motion-based spectromorphological audiovisual composition

The question

Most audiovisual works tie sound to picture mechanistically — a beat triggers a flash, a melody traces a line. Flock asks something different: what if image and sound shared an energy-motion identity rather than discrete events?

This piece is a real-time generative system in which 20,000 particles flock, merge, and form transient communities. Their collective motion is the score. The synth doesn't "play music" — it lives inside the same morphological field as the visual.

Spectromorphology

The framework comes from Denis Smalley (1997). Spectromorphology is the study of sound spectra as they unfold through time — independent of the physical source that made them. A spectromorphology is a recognisable shape of energy in time: an ascent, a sustained gesture, an oscillation, a sudden cessation.

Smalley's motion typology — unidirectional ascent / descent, cyclical motion, oscillation around a centre, layered combinations — gives us a vocabulary for talking about how energy moves through a piece without referring to "notes" or "melody". What matters is the shape of becoming.

Audiovisual counterpoint

Bret Battey extends this to audiovisual work as Fluid Audiovisual Counterpoint: sound and image as two voices that share an underlying morphological grammar but which are free to part ways and rejoin — like polyphonic voices in counterpoint. They drift independently most of the time, and at specific structural moments (a cadence) they converge.

This is more porous than the "tight sync" tradition of music video, and more meaningful than purely "atmospheric" pairing. The two media maintain their own articulation while sharing a single morphological argument.

How this work implements it

Behind the visible particles, a hidden Morphology Conductor generates an evolving energy curve in [0..1] — an ascent, a descent, a slow oscillation. By default both audio and visual sides listen to this same curve and shape their parameters along it: as energy rises, drone cutoff opens, particles brighten, granular density swells.

In counterpoint mode, a second conductor takes over the visual side. The two energy curves now drift independently. A separate Synchresis module emits periodic Gaussian cadence pulses; during each pulse the visual is pulled gently toward the audio trajectory before drifting free again.

The particles themselves are not a visualisation of the synth — they are the synth's input. Their behaviour feeds back into the sonic structure in several layers:

  • Cluster Drone: a 3D grid hash detects regions of high particle density. Each detected cluster gets a polyphonic voice (3 detuned saws → SVF lowpass → optional wave folder), and its pitch is allocated by chord-priority quantisation against the active scale. As clusters drift, voices glide.
  • Event triggers: when two particles collide and merge, a 2-op FM voice fires (Chowning-style bell), pitch drawn from the merged mass.
  • Granular cloud: 16-voice granular synth on a bundled sample (ChurchBells, from the FluCoMa Tremblay pack) whose density is driven by the live cluster count.
  • Mycelium: a dynamic Gabriel-graph of edges between nearby particles, visible as faint thread-like connections. Their count drives a pulsar synthesis click layer (Curtis Roads), giving the swarm a high-frequency rhythmic texture.
  • Wind: persistent stereo filtered noise underneath, modulated by the sum of active force-field strengths.
  • Hall reverb: a 4-tap FDN with Hadamard mixing and HF damping, giving the whole piece a unified ambient space.

Each audio layer is mapped to the conductor through its own EnergyStage — a small object specifying when along the energy axis that layer wakes up. Wind comes in early (low energy already produces a breath), drone is mid-band, brightness and metallic spectral folding only emerge at high energy. As the conductor curve rises and falls, different sonic strata enter and depart in choreographed order — the audio equivalent of an unfolding spectromorphology.

Try it

Click anywhere on the splash to begin. The control panel (top-right, click title to expand) exposes every parameter — particle fields, boid forces, mycelium link mode, synth layers, conductor curves, cadence behaviour. Drag any .wav onto the page to swap the granular source. Spacebar resets the flock; F toggles fullscreen.

The piece does not end. There is no "track length". Listening time is part of the work.
